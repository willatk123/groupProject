Introduction

First of all, in the security world there are a lot of buzz words which may seem to relate to the same thing, but in reality can be very different depending on who you ask as there are multiple organistations that set these definitions.

Key Definitions

  • Information Security - The protection of information and information systems from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction in order to provide confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
  • Cyber Security Measures and controls that ensure confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the information processed and stored by a computer.
  • Computer Security - Prevention of damage to, protection of, and restoration of computers, electronic communications systems, electronic communications services, wire communication, and electronic communication, including information contained therein, to ensure its availability, integrity, authentication, confidentiality, and nonrepudiation.
  • Confidentiality - Preserving authorized restrictions on access and disclosure, including means for protecting personal privacy and proprietary information.
  • Integrity - Guarding against improper information modification or destruction, and includes ensuring information non-repudiation and authenticity.
  • Availability - Ensuring timely and reliable access to and use of information.
  • Non-repudiation - A service that is used to provide assurance of the integrity and origin of data in such a way that the integrity and origin can be verified and validated by a third party as having originated from a specific entity.
  • IoT - Stands for Internet of Things. Used to describe any device that is connected to a network and relies on interactions with it's environment e.g. a smart thermostat. Also called Internet of Trash due to the terrible security features these devices usually have.
  • Cryptography - Branch of mathematics which focuses on obscuring information from people/ organisations who shouldn't be able to see said information through the use of complex algroithms.
  • APT - Acronym for advance persisent threat. A group/ organisation which use use advanced hacking techniques to violate the security systems of their targets. Usually backed by governments and target key industries such as banking, healthcare and military.
  • Script kiddie - An individual with a low skill set and little financial backing who uses existing scripts to exploit existing vulnerabilties.
  • Firewall - A device that monitors and filters "traffic" through the use detection systems that analyse and react to specific patterns.

All of the terms above are interlinked and the meaning of them depends on who you ask as there are multiple organisations which set the definitons of these terms. Information Security will usually include the non-technical side of security as well, whereas Cybersecurity will not. Computer security is essentially Cybersecurity but on a smaller scale.

All defintions taken from NIST which is one of main organistations which creates standards, guidelines and best practices to manage Cybersecurity/ Information Security risks. The other organistation being the ISO, hence the difference in defintions and best practices amongst other things.

Boring bit over... for now.

Threats

Threat (NIST) - Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact agency operations (including mission, functions, image, or reputation), agency assets, or individuals through an information system via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification of information, and/or denial of service.

The reasons, techniques and perpetrators of cyber crime vary massively ranging from revenge, financial gain, espionage or simply just for fun and are carried out by individuals, organisations and nations with varying degrees of damage.

Types of Attacks

  • Phishing
    • What is it - A form of social engineering where the target is tricked into performing an action which leads to a vulnerabilty.
    • How is it carried out - The attacker will usually send out emails or messages which appear to be genuine, such as a fake bank statement. This deceitful email will then trick the user into entering their bank details which are then transferred back to the attacker, allowing them to steal funds from the account.
    • How can it be prevented - Being aware of phishing attacks and taking care when clicking on links in emails/ messages is the best prevention technique here. Organisations may also introduce filters into their email system which restricts emails coming from outside sources.
  • Dos and DDoS
    • What is it - DoS and DDoS are both acronyms for Denial of Service and Distributed Denial of Service respectively. Usually directed towards organisations and governments instead of individuals as a result of ideology instead of financial gain.

    • How is it carried out - Both similar as they both overload a server with requests, resulting in reduced functionallity for that server and potentially shutting it down, hence DoS. DDoS attacks come from a network of connected computers called botnets whereas DoS attacks originate from one source. DDoS attacks are usually more punishing because of this as it can be difficult to find the origin and block it from making requests to a server.
    • How can it be prevented - By using a combination of Intrusion Detection Systems and Intrusion Prevention Systems. These basically just monitor and block any unwanted connections by matching patterns between what is actually happening and what they have been “told” what a danger is.
  • Malware Attack
    • What is it - Generic term for any attack involving malicious software. Examples include key-loggers which track what you have typed and worms which replicate themselves and infect inter-connected devices as opposed to a regular virus which usually only infect one machine.
    • How is it carried out - Usually there is an element of social engineering involved such as a phishing attack with a seemingly legitimate looking link which actually contains a malicious payload. Another way in which this is achieved is through Cross Site Scripting (XSS). To achieve this attackers will take advantage of an insecure website and sneakily embed the malicious software, which is then executed when a user accesses that website.
    • How can it be prevented - Some overlap with preventing phishing as social engineering is a key part in the delivery of malware so awareness of the way in which attackers operate is key. Anti-virus software is also a good idea. Some organisations impose restrictions on the devices their employees use. This is known as System Hardening
  • Brute Force Attack
    • What is it - A way in which an attacker “cracks” a password in order to gain unauthorized access to a system.
    • How is it carried out - Achieved through a program which normally submits between 10,000 and 1 billion passwords per second depending on how powerful their equipment is. Simple to do and very effective.
    • How can it be prevented - Using very strong passwords i.e. long, not an actual word, and containing a mix of numbers, symbols and letters. Another way which prevents unauthorized access even if the password is cracked is by using two-factor authentication (2FA) so when trying to log into a computer/ service the user will also have to enter the new factor (usually a randomly generated number).
  • SQL Injection
    • What is it SQL is an acronym for structured query language and is usually used to gather and manipulate databases. SQL Injection or SQLI is used to retrieve data such as passwords and other credentials.
    • How is it carried out - When there is the combination of website with weak security and a place where text can be inserted e.g. a login page an SQL command can be injected which either, retrives, deletes or manipulates data in the targetted database.
    • How can it be prevented - Can be prevented by placing restrictions on input fields, creating a whitelist of permitted SQL commands and maintain up to date software if possible.
  • Packet Sniffing
    • What is it A technique which in which a hacker uses a packet sniffing tool to gather information on the flow of data on a network
    • How is it carried out - Fairly simple to do and can be done at home. Simply download wireshark or some other Packet sniffing software and away you go.
    • How can it be prevented - The use of a VPN and only visiting websites that start with HTTPS would be advisable.

Case Study - Elfin

Elfin is an Iranian sponsored APT group. Also known as APT33 and Charming Kitten.

Became famous in 2012 after an attack on Saudi Arabia's state owned oil company, Aramco.

In this attack over 35,000 windows based machines were infected with the Shamoon virus causing data to be deleted and overwritten, preventing any attempt of recovery.

This resulted in the supply levels of petrol and other crude oil derivatives to reach dangerously low levels due to payment processing issues. Also left Saudi Arabia with no choice but to buy most of the world's available hard drives, further straining the current supply chain issues at the time due to floods in Thailand.

Case Study - Sandworm

Sandworm is another ATP and is a division of the Russian military. Also called Voodoo Bear.

In 2015 this group carried out an attack on Ukraine's power grid, causing power outages for ~230,000 people.

To achieve this they used BlackEnergy 3 which is the 3rd edition of the BlackEnergy malware which in it's early stages would "infect" machines allowing a botnet to be created in order to perform DDoS attacks. The latest edition has a greatly improved functionallity and includes key logging, screenshots, scan networks and the ability to remotely execute programs. In this incident the attack was spread via a sophisticated spear phishing attack, causing an employee to download the malware from an email they recieved.

Mitigation and Security Controls

The global cyber security industry is spreading like a virus with no signs of slowing down and according to these people these people, total spending on cyber security is set to reach $1.75 trillion between 2021 and 2025.

Mitigation For businesses

Although there will be some slight variation from industry to industry based on the regulations they are expected to follow there is a general approach that most companies choose to take. Can be divided into human and technical factors.

Human

The human error side is an extremely vulnerable side of cyber security, with some organistations having millions of employees it is not realistic to expect perfection. There are methods which exist to the reduce vulnerabilties introduced.

First of all, all employees need to be made aware of the risks the business face and how these risks are carried out. As covered previously, attackers can perform spear phishing attacks to either gain access to a system or to spread malware. If there is not an effective policy in place outlining staff training on the cyber security risks the onus cannot be on the employee. Other policies put in place to mitigate risk may include a clean desk policy i.e. no confidential information out in the open.

Secondly, there should policies in place detailling a incident response plan which should go outline the following steps...

  • Preparation Performing drills, policy reviews, staff training
  • Identification Time of attack, business areas affected(not usually physical locations, but possible), who discovered it.
  • Containment Making sure the attack doesn't spread to other business areas. May necessitate taking certain services offline.
  • Eradication Removing any potentially infected machines. Does the whole system need to be replaced?
  • Recovery Returning affected business areas to a trustworthy state.
  • Lessons Learned Discussion on what needs to change so this type of attack won't happen again.
